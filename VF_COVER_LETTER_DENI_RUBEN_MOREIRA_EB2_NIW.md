This section demonstrates that I meet the eligibility requirements for EB-2 classification under 8 C.F.R. § 204.5(k)(2), which defines an "advanced degree" as "any United States academic or professional degree or a foreign equivalent degree above that of baccalaureate." The regulation further provides that "a United States baccalaureate degree or a foreign equivalent degree followed by at least five years of progressive experience in the specialty shall be considered the equivalent of a master's degree."

The USCIS Policy Manual, Volume 6, Part F, Chapter 5, Section A, confirms that officers should evaluate "whether the person holds an advanced degree" or its equivalent. Per *Matter of Dhanasar*, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016), establishing EB-2 eligibility is a threshold requirement that must be addressed before the three-prong analysis of whether the national interest waiver is warranted.

Recent administrative guidance reinforces a non-discretionary approach to evaluating objective eligibility criteria. Policy Alert PA-2025-16 (August 2025) directs adjudicators to credit evidence that meets the plain regulatory elements without imposing additional subjective requirements beyond those enumerated in the regulation. This approach is consistent with *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), in which the Supreme Court held that agencies may not impose requirements beyond the text of the statute and regulation.

# PART II — PRONG 1: THE PROPOSED ENDEAVOR HAS BOTH SUBSTANTIAL MERIT AND NATIONAL IMPORTANCE

## I. Legal Framework

Under *Matter of Dhanasar*, 26 I&N Dec. 884 (AAO 2016), the first prong of the National Interest Waiver analysis requires the petitioner to demonstrate that the proposed endeavor has both *substantial merit* and *national importance*.

The AAO explained that "[s]ubstantial merit may be demonstrated in a number of areas including, but not limited to, business, entrepreneurialism, science, technology, culture, health, or education." The AAO further clarified that "[n]ational importance does not require that the endeavor have national or even regional scope" — rather, the inquiry focuses on whether the endeavor has "implications beyond the particular geographic area in which the person works."

The USCIS Policy Manual, Volume 6, Part F, Chapter 5, Section B, elaborates: "An endeavor that has significant potential to employ U.S. workers or has other substantial positive economic effects, for instance, may well be one that has substantial merit." Regarding national importance, the Policy Manual notes that "an endeavor may have national importance because it has broad implications for a field of endeavor, for example, or because of the nature of the work itself."

Policy Alert PA-2025-16 reinforces that adjudicators must evaluate the evidence presented against the plain regulatory requirements without imposing heightened standards beyond those established by precedent. *Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo*, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), further constrains the agency's ability to create extra-textual requirements through adjudicatory discretion.

# PART III — PRONG 2: I AM WELL POSITIONED TO ADVANCE THE PROPOSED ENDEAVOR

## I. Legal Framework

Under *Matter of Dhanasar*, 26 I&N Dec. 884, 889–90 (AAO 2016), the second prong requires the petitioner to demonstrate that he or she is "well positioned to advance the proposed endeavor." The AAO identified several relevant factors:

> "(a) The person's education, skills, knowledge, and record of success in related or similar efforts;
> (b) A model or plan for future activity;
> (c) Any progress towards achieving the proposed endeavor; and
> (d) The interest of potential customers, users, investors, or other relevant entities or individuals."

The USCIS Policy Manual, Volume 6, Part F, Chapter 5, Section C, further clarifies that this analysis examines "whether the person has the background, resources, and plan to succeed." The inquiry is forward-looking — it does not require that the endeavor already be operational, but rather evaluates whether the petitioner has demonstrated the capacity and preparation to carry it forward successfully.

Critically, the AAO in *Dhanasar* recognized that "progress toward the endeavor, such as the development of a business plan, the formation of a company, or the securing of funding or client commitments" constitutes relevant evidence of positioning. The formation of DRM Solutions LLC in Texas, the development of the DRM Success Framework, the commitment of $300,000 in investment capital, and the expression of client interest by Galax School Corp. collectively represent concrete, documented progress.

*Kazarian v. USCIS*, 596 F.3d 1115 (9th Cir. 2010), while primarily addressing EB-1A criteria, established the two-step analytical framework (Step 1: objective criteria; Step 2: holistic evaluation) that PA-2025-16 has since clarified should be applied non-discretionarily at Step 1. Under this framework, the evidence of education, experience, business formation, and third-party validation presented below is evaluated against the objective factors enumerated in *Dhanasar* without requiring the adjudicator to impose additional subjective standards.

# PART IV — PRONG 3: ON BALANCE, IT WOULD BE BENEFICIAL TO THE UNITED STATES TO WAIVE THE REQUIREMENTS OF A JOB OFFER AND LABOR CERTIFICATION

## I. Legal Framework

Under *Matter of Dhanasar*, 26 I&N Dec. 884, 890–91 (AAO 2016), the third prong requires a balancing analysis: whether the benefits to the United States of waiving the job offer and labor certification requirements outweigh the national interests that those requirements protect. The AAO retained the analytical factors first articulated in *New York State Department of Transportation* (NYSDOT), 22 I&N Dec. 215 (AAO 1998), as useful guides for this inquiry, while clarifying that the analysis is holistic rather than checklist-based.

The USCIS Policy Manual, Volume 6, Part F, Chapter 5, Section D, notes that "[t]he third prong involves weighing the benefits of the waiver against the national interest in protecting U.S. workers through the labor certification process." The Policy Manual further explains that "the mere fact that a position requires a specific educational background or set of skills does not, in itself, establish that the person's employment would be in the national interest" — the analysis must examine the particular circumstances of the case.

The five NYSDOT factors relevant to this analysis are: (1) the impracticability of a labor certification; (2) the benefit to the national interest even if other qualified U.S. workers are available; (3) the urgency of the matter; (4) job creation and economic impact; and (5) the nature of the self-employment and its effect on U.S. workers.

---

## PART A: FACTORS 1–3

### II. Factor 1: Impracticability of Labor Certification

The PERM labor certification process, as codified at 20 C.F.R. Part 656, presupposes a traditional employer-employee relationship in which a single employer at a fixed location seeks to hire a specific worker for a defined position. This framework is structurally incompatible with my proposed endeavor for three independent reasons:

#### A. Multi-Client Service Model

DRM Solutions LLC is a professional services firm that will simultaneously serve 10–15 client organizations per year across multiple industry sectors. The PERM process would require a single employer to sponsor my employment — but there is no single employer. I serve multiple clients through project-based engagements of varying duration (4 weeks to 18 months), with each client receiving a customized scope of services under the DRM Success Framework.

The labor certification process cannot accommodate a service model in which the beneficiary's "employer" is simultaneously 10–15 different organizations, each with different prevailing wage determinations, different metropolitan statistical areas, and different job descriptions. The requirement that PERM identify a single employer, a single location, and a single set of job duties is fundamentally incompatible with multi-client professional consulting.

#### B. Multi-State Geographic Scope

The DRM Solutions LLC target market encompasses SMEs across Texas and the broader United States. The Business Plan (**Exhibit 11**) projects operations expanding from a Texas base to regional and eventually national coverage. The PERM process ties the labor certification to a specific geographic location — a constraint that would artificially confine my services to a single metropolitan area and prevent me from serving the SMEs that most need integrated digital transformation services.

The digital gap documented in Prong 1 is national in scope — it affects 5.6 million SMEs across all 50 states. Confining my practice to a single PERM-designated location would undermine the national importance of the endeavor by restricting my ability to serve the broader market.

#### C. Self-Employment and Entrepreneurship

As the founder and principal of DRM Solutions LLC, I am both the owner and the primary service provider of the firm. The PERM process requires that the sponsoring employer be a disinterested party conducting a good-faith search for U.S. workers. A self-employed entrepreneur cannot serve as a disinterested sponsor for his own position — the regulatory fiction would be that DRM Solutions LLC is "sponsoring" its own founder, which is precisely the type of arrangement that the PERM process is not designed to accommodate.

*Matter of Dhanasar* expressly recognized that "entrepreneurs and self-employed individuals" may be eligible for the national interest waiver, acknowledging that the PERM framework is inappropriate for self-petitioning business owners. The present case is a textbook example of this principle: requiring a labor certification would either (a) prevent the formation of a new business entirely, or (b) require the artificial construction of an employer-employee relationship that does not reflect economic reality.

### III. Factor 2: Benefit Even if Qualified U.S. Workers Are Available

The national interest analysis under this factor does not ask whether qualified U.S. workers exist in the management consulting field — they clearly do. Rather, the inquiry examines whether the United States benefits from *this particular individual's* contributions even in the presence of available domestic workers.
