In a SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity), the funds are typically transferred as soon as the agreement is signed, even if the company is not yet fully established. Unlike traditional capital contributions, a SAFE does not require the company to be incorporated at the time of signing. On the other hand, a direct equity investment—where an individual injects personal capital into a company—usually takes place after the business is formally established and the investor is officially listed as a shareholder or member. This kind of investment would also typically be reflected in the investor’s income tax return, especially if the amount is substantial or if dividends are later received.
A bank loan, in contrast, requires the company to be already established and creditworthy, and the funds are disbursed only after approval. Such loans are liabilities that appear on the company’s balance sheet and may also be visible in the investor’s financial disclosures, depending on whether a personal guarantee is involved.
When it comes to proving the source of funds—particularly for immigration or regulatory purposes—investors are often required to show bank statements, tax returns, or asset declarations to demonstrate lawful possession and availability of the capital. In the case of a SAFE, even though the funds may not yet have been wired to the company, the investor must still demonstrate financial capability, typically through recent bank statements or a tax return that reflects sufficient assets or income. This financial transparency helps verify that the investment is legitimate and not derived from unlawful sources.
